Electrochemistry Formulas

1. Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

Formula: \( E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cathode}} - E^\circ_{\text{anode}} \)

Where:

  • E°cell: Standard electrode potential of the cell (in volts, V)
  • E°cathode: Standard reduction potential of the cathode (in volts, V)
  • E°anode: Standard reduction potential of the anode (in volts, V)

If E°cell is positive, the reaction is spontaneous; if negative, the reaction is non-spontaneous.

2. Nernst Equation

Formula: \( E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q \)

At standard conditions (298K): \( E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{0.0592}{n} \log Q \)

Where:

  • Ecell: Cell potential under non-standard conditions (in volts, V)
  • E°cell: Standard electrode potential (in volts, V)
  • R: Universal gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin, K)
  • n: Number of moles of electrons transferred
  • F: Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol)
  • Q: Reaction quotient, \( \frac{[\text{products}]}{[\text{reactants}]} \)

3. Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

First Law: The amount of substance deposited or liberated at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

Formula: \( m = \frac{Q \times M}{n \times F} \)

Where:

  • m: Mass of the substance deposited or liberated (in grams)
  • Q: Total electric charge passed (in coulombs, C)
  • M: Molar mass of the substance (in g/mol)
  • n: Number of moles of electrons required to deposit or liberate one mole of the substance
  • F: Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol)

Second Law: The masses of different substances deposited or liberated by the same quantity of electricity are proportional to their equivalent weights.

4. Relationship Between Gibbs Free Energy and Cell Potential

Formula: \( \Delta G = -nFE_{\text{cell}} \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta G\): Gibbs free energy change (in joules, J)
  • n: Number of moles of electrons transferred
  • F: Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol)
  • Ecell: Cell potential (in volts, V)

If \(\Delta G\) is negative, the reaction is spontaneous.

5. Electrochemical Cell Notation

General Notation:

Anode | Anode Solution (concentration) || Cathode Solution (concentration) | Cathode

Example:

Zn(s) | Zn2+(1 M) || Cu2+(1 M) | Cu(s)

This notation represents the setup of an electrochemical cell, where the anode is on the left and the cathode is on the right.

6. Conductivity (\(\kappa\))

Formula: \( \kappa = \frac{1}{\rho} \)

Where:

  • \(\kappa\): Conductivity of the solution (in S/m)
  • \(\rho\): Resistivity of the solution (in ohm·meters, Ω·m)

7. Molar Conductivity (Λm)

Formula: \( \Lambda_m = \frac{\kappa}{C} \)

Where:

  • Λm: Molar conductivity (in S·m²/mol)
  • κ: Conductivity of the solution (in S/m)
  • C: Concentration of the solution (in mol/m³)
