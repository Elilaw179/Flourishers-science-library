Chemical Equilibrium Formulas

1. Equilibrium Constant (Kc)

Formula: \( K_c = \frac{[\text{products}]}{[\text{reactants}]} \)

Where:

  • Kc: Equilibrium constant for concentrations
  • [products]: Concentration of the products (raised to the power of their coefficients)
  • [reactants]: Concentration of the reactants (raised to the power of their coefficients)

This formula applies to a reversible reaction at equilibrium, such as:

\( aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD \)

The equilibrium constant expression would be:

\( K_c = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b} \)

2. Equilibrium Constant (Kp) for Gases

Formula: \( K_p = \frac{P_{\text{products}}}{P_{\text{reactants}}} \)

Where:

  • Kp: Equilibrium constant for partial pressures
  • Pproducts: Partial pressure of the products (raised to the power of their coefficients)
  • Preactants: Partial pressure of the reactants (raised to the power of their coefficients)

This formula applies to reactions involving gases at equilibrium.

3. Relationship Between Kc and Kp

Formula: \( K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n} \)

Where:

  • Kp: Equilibrium constant for partial pressures
  • Kc: Equilibrium constant for concentrations
  • R: Universal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • \(\Delta n\): Change in the number of moles of gas (\( \Delta n = \text{moles of gaseous products} - \text{moles of gaseous reactants} \))

4. Reaction Quotient (Q)

Formula: \( Q = \frac{[\text{products}]}{[\text{reactants}]} \)

Where:

  • Q: Reaction quotient, which is calculated like Kc or Kp but under any conditions, not just at equilibrium.
  • [products]: Concentration or partial pressure of the products at any given moment
  • [reactants]: Concentration or partial pressure of the reactants at any given moment

Comparison between Q and K:

  • If \( Q < K \), the reaction will proceed in the forward direction to reach equilibrium.
  • If \( Q > K \), the reaction will proceed in the reverse direction to reach equilibrium.
  • If \( Q = K \), the reaction is at equilibrium.

5. Le Chatelier's Principle

Principle: If a dynamic equilibrium is disturbed by changing the conditions, the position of equilibrium moves to counteract the change.

Le Chatelier's Principle helps predict the direction in which the equilibrium will shift when conditions such as concentration, pressure, or temperature are changed.

6. Gibbs Free Energy and Equilibrium (ΔG and K)

Formula: \( \Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q \)

At equilibrium (\( \Delta G = 0 \) and \( Q = K \)):

Formula: \( \Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta G\): Gibbs free energy change (in joules or kilojoules)
  • \(\Delta G^\circ\): Standard Gibbs free energy change (in joules or kilojoules)
  • R: Universal gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • K: Equilibrium constant
  • Q: Reaction quotient

If \( \Delta G^\circ < 0 \), the reaction is spontaneous and favors the formation of products.

If \( \Delta G^\circ > 0 \), the reaction is non-spontaneous and favors the formation of reactants.
