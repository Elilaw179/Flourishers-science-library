Redox Reactions Formulas

1. Oxidation Number (State)

General Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers:

  • The oxidation number of an element in its free (uncombined) state is zero. Example: \( \text{O}_2 \), \( \text{H}_2 \).
  • The oxidation number of a monatomic ion is equal to the charge of the ion. Example: \( \text{Na}^+ \) has an oxidation number of +1.
  • Oxygen usually has an oxidation number of -2, except in peroxides (e.g., \( \text{H}_2\text{O}_2 \)) where it is -1.
  • Hydrogen usually has an oxidation number of +1 when bonded to non-metals and -1 when bonded to metals.
  • Fluorine has an oxidation number of -1 in all of its compounds.
  • The sum of the oxidation numbers in a neutral compound is zero. In a polyatomic ion, the sum equals the ion's charge.

2. Oxidation and Reduction

Oxidation: Loss of electrons or increase in oxidation number.

Reduction: Gain of electrons or decrease in oxidation number.

Example:

  • Oxidation: \( \text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^- \) (Zinc loses 2 electrons)
  • Reduction: \( \text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \) (Copper gains 2 electrons)

3. Balancing Redox Reactions

Steps for Balancing Redox Reactions in Acidic Solution:

  1. Assign oxidation numbers to all atoms in the reaction.
  2. Identify and separate the oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
  3. Balance all atoms except hydrogen and oxygen.
  4. Balance oxygen atoms by adding \( \text{H}_2\text{O} \).
  5. Balance hydrogen atoms by adding \( \text{H}^+ \).
  6. Balance the charge by adding electrons (\( e^- \)).
  7. Make the number of electrons equal in both half-reactions by multiplying the half-reactions by appropriate coefficients.
  8. Add the half-reactions together and simplify to get the balanced redox equation.

4. Redox Reaction in Basic Solution

For reactions in a basic solution, after balancing the redox reaction as if it were in an acidic solution, add \( \text{OH}^- \) ions to both sides of the equation for each \( \text{H}^+ \) ion present. Combine \( \text{H}^+ \) and \( \text{OH}^- \) to form \( \text{H}_2\text{O} \), then simplify the equation if necessary.

5. Cell Potential (Ecell) in Redox Reactions

Formula: \( E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cathode}} - E^\circ_{\text{anode}} \)

Where:

  • Ecell: Cell potential or electromotive force (EMF) of the cell (in volts, V)
  • E°cathode: Standard reduction potential of the cathode (in volts, V)
  • E°anode: Standard reduction potential of the anode (in volts, V)

If Ecell is positive, the redox reaction is spontaneous.

6. Relationship Between Free Energy and Cell Potential

Formula: \( \Delta G = -nFE_{\text{cell}} \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta G\): Gibbs free energy change (in joules, J)
  • n: Number of moles of electrons transferred in the reaction
  • F: Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol)
  • Ecell: Cell potential (in volts, V)

If \(\Delta G\) is negative, the reaction is spontaneous.

7. Faraday's Law of Electrolysis

Formula: \( m = \frac{Q \times M}{n \times F} \)

Where:

  • m: Mass of the substance deposited or liberated (in grams)
  • Q: Total electric charge passed (in coulombs, C)
  • M: Molar mass of the substance (in g/mol)
  • n: Number of moles of electrons required to deposit or liberate one mole of the substance
  • F: Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol)

8. Electrochemical Cell Notation

General Notation:

Anode | Anode Solution (concentration) || Cathode Solution (concentration) | Cathode

Example:

Zn(s) | Zn2+(1 M) || Cu2+(1 M) | Cu(s)

This notation represents the setup of an electrochemical cell, where the anode is on the left and the cathode is on the right.
