Thermochemistry Formulas

1. Heat (q)

Formula: \( q = mc\Delta T \)

Where:

  • q: Heat absorbed or released (in joules, J)
  • m: Mass of the substance (in grams, g)
  • c: Specific heat capacity of the substance (in J/g°C)
  • \(\Delta T\): Change in temperature (in °C or K)

This formula is used to calculate the amount of heat absorbed or released by a substance during a temperature change.

2. Enthalpy Change (ΔH)

Formula: \( \Delta H = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}} \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta H\): Change in enthalpy (in joules, J or kilojoules, kJ)
  • Hproducts: Enthalpy of the products
  • Hreactants: Enthalpy of the reactants

If \(\Delta H\) is negative, the reaction is exothermic (releases heat). If \(\Delta H\) is positive, the reaction is endothermic (absorbs heat).

3. Hess's Law

Formula: \( \Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 + \dots \)

Principle: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes for each step in the reaction, regardless of the path taken.

4. Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔHf°)

Formula: \( \Delta H^\circ_{\text{reaction}} = \sum \Delta H^\circ_f(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H^\circ_f(\text{reactants}) \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta H^\circ_f\): Standard enthalpy of formation of a substance
  • The standard enthalpy of formation of an element in its standard state is zero.

This formula is used to calculate the enthalpy change of a reaction using standard enthalpies of formation.

5. Calorimetry

Formula: \( q_{\text{system}} = -q_{\text{surroundings}} \)

This principle is used in calorimetry to measure the heat exchanged in a chemical reaction or physical process.

6. Bomb Calorimetry

Formula: \( q_{\text{reaction}} = -C_{\text{cal}} \times \Delta T \)

Where:

  • qreaction: Heat of the reaction (in joules, J)
  • Ccal: Heat capacity of the calorimeter (in J/°C)
  • \(\Delta T\): Change in temperature (in °C)

This formula is used in bomb calorimetry, where the heat of a combustion reaction is measured.

7. Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

Formula: \( \Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta G\): Gibbs free energy change (in joules, J or kilojoules, kJ)
  • \(\Delta H\): Enthalpy change (in joules, J or kilojoules, kJ)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin, K)
  • \(\Delta S\): Entropy change (in J/K)

Interpretation: If \(\Delta G\) is negative, the reaction is spontaneous. If \(\Delta G\) is positive, the reaction is non-spontaneous.

8. Relationship Between ΔG° and Equilibrium Constant (K)

Formula: \( \Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta G^\circ\): Standard Gibbs free energy change (in joules, J or kilojoules, kJ)
  • R: Universal gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
  • T: Temperature (in Kelvin, K)
  • K: Equilibrium constant
